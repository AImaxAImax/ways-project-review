# Visual Direction v3 — Say Dog, See Dog

## Problem found in v2
The v2 Comfy pass has a consistent mood, but too many shots resolve to the same idea: a calm shark underwater. That makes the video feel visually repetitive, even if each image is technically different.

## New rule
**Say dog, see dog.**

**2026-05 v21 reassessment:** museum/exhibit language is now constrained to evidence/proof beats. The short should not feel like every frame happens in the same museum room. Keep the premium natural-history finish, but vary the worlds: ancient ocean, barren coast, tabletop comparison, geologic timeline, primitive shoreline, aquarium viewer, final ocean.

Every narrated idea must have a distinct, literal visual payload. If the script says trees, we need trees or clearly tree-like absence/arrival. If it says apples, we need apples. If it says squirrels, we need a squirrel/nut gag. If it says 400+ million years, we need time/deep-time visual language. The shark should not be the subject of every frame.

## v3 shot map

### 1. “Sharks were here before trees.”
**Visual job:** immediate hook + impossible comparison.
**Image:** split-scale composition: ancient shark underwater on the left/bottom, empty barren land above/right with no trees. A ghostly absent forest outline can be implied, but no actual modern trees yet.
**Different because:** not just shark portrait; it visually says “before trees.”

### 2. “Seriously.”
**Visual job:** pause / comedic confirmation.
**Image:** simple museum placard / fossil exhibit setup: shark fossil silhouette, stamped “yep” feeling, maybe a tiny skeptical icon-like face, but no readable AI text in image; captions handle the word.
**Different because:** graphic/museum proof beat, not cinematic ocean.

### 3. “Before forests. Before apples. Before squirrels had anywhere to hide nuts.”
**Visual job:** literal list gag.
**Image:** playful prehistoric checklist scene: empty land with transparent/ghosted forest, apple, and squirrel/nut icons floating above it as “not yet invented” objects. No readable text inside image.
**Different because:** land/comedy/list visual, not ocean.

### 4. “The first sharks showed up more than four hundred million years ago.”
**Visual job:** deep time.
**Image:** vertical geologic timeline / museum diorama style: glowing ancient ocean at bottom with shark silhouette, stacked fossil layers/clocks/rock strata rising upward. Captions carry “400+ million.”
**Different because:** time/layers, not animal portrait.

### 5. “Trees took their time. They didn’t really arrive until tens of millions of years later.”
**Visual job:** trees finally arrive, but late.
**Image:** primitive early forest / tree-like plants emerging above a shoreline, while the shark remains below water in the background. Make the land/trees the hero, not the shark.
**Different because:** first true plant/forest arrival shot.

### 6. “So when you look at a shark, you’re not just looking at an animal.”
**Visual job:** reframe the shark as ancient artifact.
**Image:** modern kid/family safe aquarium or museum silhouette looking at a shark through glass, with subtle fossil/ancient ocean reflection overlay.
**Different because:** viewer perspective / modern-to-ancient bridge.

### 7. “You’re looking at a survivor from a world before forests.”
**Visual job:** final awe.
**Image:** return to the calm shot-4 style: small shark silhouette in vast glowing ancient ocean, but with faint ghostly pre-forest shoreline above. This can be the one pure awe shark frame.
**Different because:** earned final callback, not repeated six times.

## Prompting changes
- Use varied subject anchors: ocean, barren land, museum exhibit, checklist gag, timeline, early forest, aquarium viewer, final ocean.
- Use varied composition types: split scene, graphic exhibit, comic checklist, geological timeline, plant diorama, viewer POV, final cinematic wide.
- Keep unified style through palette and rendering, not through repeating the same shark portrait.
- Negative prompt: no readable text, no logos, no gore, no horror, no attack pose, no scary teeth, no malformed shark mouth.

## Acceptance checklist
- Each still should be identifiable by concept even before captions.
- At least three shots should not have a shark as the main subject.
- The squirrel/apple beat should be visually funny and literal.
- The timeline beat should visually communicate deep time.
- The final beat can reuse the calm prehistoric ocean vibe, but only as the emotional landing.
